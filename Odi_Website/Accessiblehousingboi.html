Accessibility-Inclusive Design for Rural Cabin

Making an accessible cabin in the Appalachian Mountains was an exciting prospect for me. Living off-the-grid is not easy and especially not in the terrain of Woodland Harvest Mountain Farm, but it is an extremely beautiful place to live and reflect in. I wanted to open the opportunity for a variety of visitors to come to Woodland Harvest Mountain Farm. My codesigner Mia Skaggs and I first started by identifying the stakeholders and becomed well-versed in the language of universal design.

When we started designing the cabin and the interior, many factors influenced our decision. The Appalachian Mountains have intense shade coverage, meaning that the cabin should have as much passive solar heating. This means that during the winter, our visitor doesnâ€™t have to chop up wood for a wood stove or always pour propane in their propane heater throughout the day.

Therefore, windows and skylight were mainly pointed towards the South, where Winter sun is most prominent. Triangle nooks on the south side and east side were style-based, as they would make good spaces for shelves or small seating areas.

The door of the cabin is facing towards a downward incline, making it easy to construct a ramp with a max of 20 degree incline, or lower if the incline is leveled out through manual digging.

When we started building, the farm hosts guided us on properly making a pole barn foundation and wall frame construction. Our frames were designed to provide as much solar heating as possible and with windows low enough for someone in a wheelchair to open and close them.

Woodland Harvest Mountain Farm has made it possible for me to continue improving this cabin and actualizing my design of porches, ramps, and interior living design for Spring 2021.
